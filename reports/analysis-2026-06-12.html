Key Qualification
This is not a traditional FDA/earnings catalyst. Index inclusion is mechanical — it triggers forced buying by passive ETFs regardless of SpaceX's business performance. The uncertainty isn't "will SpaceX succeed?" but rather "is the inclusion timeline and demand impact already priced into the $135 IPO price?"

The market had weeks to price in this inclusion. With a 4x oversubscribed IPO, most of the inclusion premium may already be baked in. This dramatically reduces the post-inclusion pop compared to a stock that started trading before the inclusion rule existed.
5 — Analog Analysis
Saudi Aramco (2222.SE)

Setup similarity: Prior record holder for largest IPO ($29.4B raised, $1.88T market cap). Massive hype, fixed-price IPO, national pride narrative. Oversubscribed. Market expected a pop on debut.

Outcome: Rose 10% first two days, then spent years oscillating around IPO price. Long-term performance poor vs. S&P 500.
Key difference: Aramco has earnings; SpaceX is GAAP negative and spending $20B+ annually on Starship. Capex drag is more severe.
Meta / Facebook (META)
2012 IPO
−52% within 4 months → then 40x
Setup similarity: "Biggest tech IPO ever" narrative, massive retail demand, fixed price. Dropped below IPO price within days and spent 14 months underwater. Then went on to 40x over the following decade.

Outcome: Near-term: brutal for IPO buyers. Long-term: generational compounder. The distinction is that Meta was already profitable at IPO.
Key difference: Facebook was profitable and growing EPS at IPO. SpaceX is structurally GAAP negative and likely remains so for years while Starship matures.
Alibaba (BABA)
2014 IPO
+38% day 1 → −40% within 12 months
Setup similarity: Then-record IPO ($25B raised), +38% first day pop on enormous investor demand, "once-in-a-generation" company narrative. Then reality hit: high valuation at launch, first lock-up expiration created sustained selling.

Outcome: Day-1 buyers experienced a 40% drawdown before recovery. Pattern: mega-hype IPO → post-lockup de-rating → eventual recovery over years.
Key difference: BABA was highly profitable at IPO. SpaceX analog differs because the lockup structure is tiered and gentler — but the supply overhang theme holds.
Analog conclusion: Every mega-IPO analog shows the same pattern — near-term pop (1–4 weeks), followed by significant drawdown as lockup tranches hit. The "right" time to buy SpaceX in each analog was 6–18 months post-IPO, not day one.
